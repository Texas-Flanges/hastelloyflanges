# How to Install Hastelloy Flanges: Best Practices Guide

Proper installation is just as important as material selection when it comes to **Hastelloy flanges**. Even the highest-quality flanges will fail if installed incorrectly. This comprehensive guide covers professional installation techniques, from welding to final torque, ensuring your critical connections perform reliably for decades.

## Pre-Installation Planning

### Review Design Documents
Before starting installation:
- Verify flange specifications match P&IDs
- Confirm pressure class and facing type
- Check material certifications
- Review welding procedures

### Inspect Materials
Examine **Hastelloy flanges** for:
- Shipping damage
- Surface defects
- Correct markings and heat numbers
- Proper dimensions

### Prepare Work Area
Ensure adequate:
- Lighting for welding
- Ventilation (nickel fumes require good air circulation)
- Access for torquing equipment
- Storage for components

## Weld Neck Flange Installation

### Step 1: Pipe Preparation

**Cut pipe square:**
- Use appropriate cutting method (saw, plasma, grinder)
- Keep cut perpendicular to pipe axis (within 1/16" per foot)
- Avoid cold working the material excessively

**Bevel preparation:**
- Standard V-groove: 37.5° ±2.5° per side
- Root face: 1/16" ±1/32"
- Remove all mill scale and oxidation

**Cleanliness is critical:**
- Wire brush to bright metal
- Degrease with approved solvent
- Remove all moisture before welding

### Step 2: Fit-Up

**Align components:**
- Check flange face perpendicularity
- Verify bolt holes straddle or align as required
- Maintain consistent root gap (typically 1/16" to 3/32")

**Tack welding:**
- Use qualified Hastelloy filler metal (ERNiCrMo-4 for C276)
- Place 3-4 tacks equally spaced
- Keep tacks small (1/2" to 3/4" long)
- Allow to cool between tacks

### Step 3: Welding Hastelloy

**Critical welding parameters:**

**GTAW (TIG) - Preferred method:**
- Current: 150-200 amps (6" Sch 40)
- Argon back-purge REQUIRED
- Oxygen content <50 ppm in purge
- Interpass temperature: Keep below 350°F

**GMAW (MIG) - Production alternative:**
- 98% Ar / 2% O2 shielding gas
- Lower travel speeds than steel
- Maintain adequate shielding coverage

**Key practices:**
- **Never** use chlorinated cleaners before welding
- Avoid excessive heat input (causes sensitization)
- Stringer beads preferred over weave patterns
- Remove oxide scale between passes (stainless wire brush only)

**Post-weld treatment:**
- Light grinding to blend transitions
- Pickle welds with citric or nitric acid paste (if required)
- Final passivation per ASTM A967

### Step 4: Weld Inspection

Visual inspection for:
- Complete fusion
- No undercut
- Smooth profile
- No cracks or porosity

Non-destructive testing (if specified):
- Liquid penetrant (PT)
- Radiography (RT)
- Ultrasonic (UT)

## Slip-On Flange Installation

### Advantages
- Easier alignment
- Lower weld skill requirement
- Faster installation

### Limitations
- Two fillet welds required (inside and outside)
- Not recommended for severe cyclic service
- Lower fatigue life than weld neck

### Installation Steps

**Slide flange onto pipe:**
- Position flange face at correct distance from pipe end
- Typically 1/8" gap for expansion

**Inside fillet weld:**
- Critical for pressure integrity
- Must achieve full throat thickness
- Use mirror to verify quality

**Outside fillet weld:**
- Seals against external corrosion
- Provides additional strength
- Must blend smoothly to flange face

## Socket Weld Flange Installation

**Special considerations for small bore:**

**Gap requirement:**
- Insert pipe to bottom of socket
- Withdraw 1/16" before welding
- This gap prevents cracking from thermal expansion

**Fillet weld:**
- Single external fillet
- Full throat thickness
- Smooth profile essential

**Hastelloy advantage:**
- Excellent fatigue resistance in vibration applications
- Ideal for instrument connections

## Gasket Selection and Installation

### Compatible Gasket Materials

**For Hastelloy flanges:**

**PTFE (Teflon):**
- Excellent chemical compatibility
- Temperature: -400°F to 500°F
- Low bolt load requirements
- Good for glass-lined mating surfaces

**Flexible graphite:**
- Outstanding temperature range: -400°F to 850°F
- Good sealability
- Handles thermal cycling
- May require initial retorque

**Spiral wound with graphite filler:**
- High-pressure capability
- Excellent resilience
- Hastelloy inner/outer rings for corrosion resistance
- Industry standard for critical service

**CAF (Compressed Asbestos-Free):**
- General purpose
- Good for lower temperatures
- Economic choice for non-critical service

### Gasket Installation

**Surface preparation:**
- Clean flange faces completely
- Remove old gasket material
- Check for surface damage
- Inspect for warping or erosion

**Gasket centering:**
- Center gasket on bolt circle
- Ensure equal overlap inside and outside
- Use centering ring if provided
- Never reuse spiral wound gaskets

## Bolt Installation and Torque

### Bolt Material Selection

**For corrosive service with Hastelloy flanges:**

**B7 studs with PTFE nuts:**
- Standard for moderate temperatures
- Cost-effective
- Adequate for most applications

**B7M (A193) - Modified material:**
- Better wet H2S resistance
- Preferred for sour service

**Hastelloy bolts (A453 Grade 660):**
- Maximum corrosion resistance
- Required for some severe services
- Expensive but eliminates galvanic concerns

**Nickel alloy (A320 L7M):**
- Low-temperature applications
- Excellent toughness

### Lubrication

**Critical for proper torque:**
- Use anti-seize compatible with process
- Nickel-based anti-seize for **Hastelloy flanges**
- Apply to threads and under nut face
- Torque values assume lubrication

### Torque Sequence

**Pattern:**
- Start with four-bolt cross pattern
- Proceed in star/criss-cross pattern
- Never tighten adjacent bolts sequentially

**Steps:**
1. Hand-tighten all bolts
2. Tighten to 30% of final torque
3. Tighten to 60% of final torque
4. Tighten to 100% of final torque
5. Repeat 100% pass

**Torque values (6" Class 300, 3/4" bolt):**
- With lubrication: 180 ft-lbs
- Dry (not recommended): 270 ft-lbs

*Always follow manufacturer specifications for your specific flange*

### Torque Verification

**Immediately after installation:**
- Verify all bolts to specification
- Check for uniform gasket compression
- Look for flange misalignment

**After pressurization (24-48 hours):**
- Retorque to compensate for gasket creep
- Especially important with soft gaskets
- Normal to lose 10-20% of initial torque

## Special Installation Considerations

### High-Temperature Service

**Thermal expansion compensation:**
- Account for differential expansion
- Use proper pipe supports
- Consider expansion joints
- Verify cold spring requirements

**Hot bolt torque:**
- May require retorque at operating temperature
- Use long studs with nuts outside insulation
- Document hot torque values

### Cryogenic Service

**Low-temperature concerns:**
- Verify bolt material has adequate impact toughness
- Use proper insulation methods
- Prevent ice formation on bolts
- Monitor for frost heaving effects

### Vacuum Service

**Special requirements:**
- Higher torque for metal gaskets
- Helium leak testing often required
- Surface finish more critical
- Avoid spiral wound (may leak helium)

## Post-Installation Testing

### Pressure Testing

**Hydrostatic test:**
- Typically 1.5× design pressure
- Hold for specified duration
- Inspect all flanges for leaks

**Pneumatic test (if hydro not feasible):**
- Use extreme caution (stored energy hazard)
- Never exceed 25% of hydrostatic test pressure
- Evacuate area during pressure-up

### Leak Detection

**Methods:**
- Bubble solution (soap)
- Ultrasonic leak detector
- Halogen sniffer
- Infrared camera

## Common Installation Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Inadequate Purge

**Problem:** Sugaring on root pass due to oxidation
**Result:** Weak weld, potential crevice corrosion
**Solution:** Maintain argon purge until weld cools

### Mistake 2: Excessive Heat Input

**Problem:** Sensitization of Hastelloy
**Result:** Intergranular corrosion in weld zone
**Solution:** Control interpass temperature, use stringer beads

### Mistake 3: Incorrect Torque

**Problem:** Either under-torque (leaks) or over-torque (gasket damage)
**Result:** Failed seal
**Solution:** Use calibrated torque wrench, follow sequence

### Mistake 4: Misaligned Flanges

**Problem:** Bolt holes don't align, gasket compressed unevenly
**Result:** Premature gasket failure, bolt fatigue
**Solution:** Proper pipe support, accurate fit-up

### Mistake 5: Contaminated Surfaces

**Problem:** Oil, grease, or old gasket material on faces
**Result:** Leaks despite proper torque
**Solution:** Clean thoroughly before assembly

## Maintenance and Inspection Schedule

### Initial Period (First 6 months)
- Check torque monthly
- Inspect for leaks weekly
- Document any issues

### Normal Operation
- Retorque annually (or per plant standards)
- Inspect during turnarounds
- Replace gaskets per schedule

### Documentation Requirements

Maintain records of:
- Flange material and heat number
- Gasket type and manufacturer
- Bolt material and sizes
- Torque values applied
- Inspection results
- Any repairs or modifications

## Safety Considerations

**Personal protective equipment:**
- Welding helmet with proper shade
- Leather gloves and jacket
- Respirator for nickel fume exposure
- Safety glasses

**Process safety:**
- Verify isolation before breaking flanges
- Use proper lockout/tagout
- Test for presence of process material
- Have spill response ready
